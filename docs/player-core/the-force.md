# The Force

*She closed her eyes, and the blaster bolts slowed — not in truth, but in her knowing. A breath before each one was fired, she already knew where it would go, and where she would not be.*

The Force is the energy field that binds the galaxy together, and those sensitive to it can bend it to their will — pushing minds and matter, foreseeing the moment before it comes, mending or unmaking flesh. In this game the Force is **not** a list of spells you ration through the day. A Force user is a *doer*: every power is an **action you take in the moment**, fueled by your **Attunement** and channeled through your class's framework. You don't "have spells left" — you have a focus that ebbs and refills across a fight.

This chapter explains how Force powers work, lays out the **six Applications** they're organized into, lists every power by level, and then describes each one in full.

---

## The Six Applications of the Force

Every power belongs to one **Application** — the discipline of the Force it draws on. The Applications come in two traditions of three, the Jedi triad mirrored by the Sith triad:

**The Jedi triad**
- **Control** — self-mastery, and the control of energy: barriers, absorption, deflection, the stilling of one's own fear.
- **Sense** — precognition and perception: foreseeing attacks, viewing distant places, piercing illusion.
- **Alter** — kinetic manipulation: moving, lifting, throwing, and crushing the physical world.

**The Sith triad**
- **Body** — augmenting the flesh: vitality, healing, draining life, defying death itself.
- **Mind** — the domination of others: influence, fear, control, and the unmaking of memory.
- **Offense** — raw destruction: lightning, energy, and annihilation.

> **Sidebar — Tradition Is Emphasis, Not a Wall**
> The triads describe where a power *comes from*, not who's allowed to use it — access is earned with feats, not gated by alignment. The **Body** Application holds both the healer's Vital Transfer and the dark Drain Life; **Mind** holds both the benign mind trick and the monstrous Dominate Mind. What marks true corruption is the **[dark]** trait on a power, not its Application. (A future corruption subsystem will give [dark] powers teeth; for now, the trait flags them.)

## How Force Powers Work

### Attunement — your focus in the fight

Using powers spends **Attunement**, a pool that refreshes **each encounter** (there is no daily total — see *Playing the Game*). Your pool is **2 + your Level Bonus** points, and you can spend an action on **Center** (`[one-action]`) to regain **2 Attunement**, steadying yourself mid-fight. When the fight ends, your Attunement resets to full.

Powers cost Attunement by tier:

| Tier | Cost | What lives here |
|---|---|---|
| **Basic** | **0** | At-will utility — sensing life, gripping an object, deflecting a bolt. Limited only by your actions. |
| **Standard** | **1–2** | The workhorses — your attacks, saves, and buffs. |
| **Greater** | **3** | The tier-breakers — scaled control, mass effects, resurrection, area devastation (available only at higher levels). |

> A power with **combat impact always costs at least 1 Attunement.** The 0-cost powers are deliberately minor utility, so they can't be the whole fight.

### Your Force DC and your rolls

When a power lets a foe resist, they roll a save against your **Force DC**:

> **Force DC = 10 + Level Bonus + your Force proficiency + your key attribute.**

When a power makes an *attack*, you roll `d20 + Level Bonus + Force proficiency + key attribute` against the target's defense, just like any other check. Your key attribute is set by your class (a Consular's Charisma, a Sentinel's Wisdom, and so on).

### Acquiring powers — bought, not granted

Powers are a **shared, portable toolkit**. You learn them by spending **feats**, choosing from the Applications your class and feats give you access to — **no power is locked to one class.** What your class provides is your *framework* and *emphasis*: how many powers you start with, which Applications come easily, and the verb you channel them through (the Consular's deep Manifest, the Guardian's blade, the Sentinel's premonitions). A non-Force class can even pick up a few powers through the Force Adept archetype.

Each power lists a **level** — the earliest level you can learn it (1, 5, 9, 13, or 17). The highest-tier powers simply aren't available until you've grown into them.

### Scaling — powers grow with you

You never have to re-learn a power to keep it relevant. A damage power gains **+1 die at 5th, 11th, and 17th level**, so a power learned at 1st level arcs from one die up to four across a career — right alongside the weapon-damage curve. **Force damage never adds your attribute modifier**; a power buys you *range, area, or a save to target*, not raw damage above what a weapon already does. (Flat effects like a barrier's resistance scale on your Level Bonus instead.)

### Push — pay one more for something more

Many powers have a **Push** upgrade: spend **1 extra Attunement** as you use the power to add an effect. A Push always adds *something new* — a redirect, an extra condition, a second target — **never just a bigger number.** A power's Push line tells you what you get.

### Control, reactions, and cooldowns

- **Hard control** — powers that take a creature out of the fight (hold, paralysis, domination) — carry the **[incapacitation]** trait (a higher-level target shrugs the result one degree better) **and** the **[once per minute]** trait, so you can't pour a whole pool into repeated save-or-lose. Soft control (frightened, fascinated) has no such cap.
- On any control power, a **critical failure** makes the effect *worse* (a harsher condition, a longer duration) — but it **never** removes a creature from the fight forever or kills it outright. Drama, not deletion.
- Some powers are **reactions** `[reaction]`, used on others' turns when their trigger fires (and counting against your three-reactions-per-round limit). Others you **Sustain** (spend an action on a later turn to keep them going).

## Reading a Power

Every power entry follows the same shape. Once you've read one, you can read them all:

> **Power Name**  *(action cost)*  **·  Power [level]**
> **Traits** Force, *(its Application)*, *(any other tags)*
> **Cost** *(Attunement)*  ·  **Range / Area / Targets** *(reach and what it affects)*
> **Defense** *(the save or defense it's rolled against)*  ·  **Duration** *(how long it lasts)*
> ———
> The effect, including the four degrees of success when a save or attack is involved.
> **Push (+1 Attunement)** the upgrade, if any.

> **Example — Reading Force Push**
> *The entry header reads **Force Push [two-actions] · Power 1**, with **Traits** Force, Alter and **Cost** 1 Attunement. Its **Range** is 30 feet at a single creature, its **Defense** a basic Fortitude save. The effect lists what happens on each degree of the target's save, and its **Push** lets you catch a whole cone of foes instead of one. That's every field you'll see, in the order you'll always see them.*
