# Appendix: Conditions

*The medbay droid ran its scanner down her arm and chirped the verdict: two cracked ribs, mild plasma burns, and a concussion that would blur her aim for a day. "You're fatigued, enfeebled, and lucky," her captain translated. "Sleep it off."*

Conditions are the standard ways the game changes what a creature can do: the blinding flash, the crushing grip, the fear that shakes your aim. When an effect makes you **frightened 2** or **prone**, this appendix tells you exactly what that means. Each condition is defined once, here — the ability that gives you the condition tells you *when* you get it and how long it lasts; this appendix tells you *what it does*.

**Condition values.** Some conditions have a numerical value after their name, such as frightened 2. The value tells you how strong the condition is — usually the size of a penalty — and the entry explains how the value changes. If an effect would give you a condition you already have, use the higher value; the values don't add together.

**Conditions don't stack with themselves.** You can have many *different* conditions at once (and some conditions give you others — grabbed makes you off-guard and immobilized), but two instances of the *same* condition aren't cumulative: apply the more severe one. If two effects impose the same condition for different durations, the condition ends when the last remaining duration does.

**How conditions end.** The effect that gave you a condition states its duration. Some conditions instead say how they end in their own entry (frightened counts down; dying uses recovery checks). If nothing says otherwise, a condition lasts as long as its source does.

**Penalties from conditions follow the usual stacking rules.** Most condition penalties are **status** penalties (a few, noted in their entries, are circumstance penalties), so they don't stack with other status penalties — only the worst applies.

> **Sidebar — Death and Dying**
> Four conditions interlock to govern falling in battle: **dying** (you're at death's door, tracked with recovery checks), **wounded** (you've been dying recently, and it gets worse if it happens again), **doomed** (death is closer than it should be), and **unconscious** (you're out cold, dying or not). Each is defined in its entry below; together they replace any single "death saves" rule.

> **Sidebar — Degrees of Detection**
> Five conditions form a ladder describing how well a creature perceives you: **observed** (in plain view) → **concealed** (visible but obscured — DC 5 flat check to target you) → **hidden** (they know your square, not your position — DC 11 flat check) → **undetected** (they don't know where you are) → **unnoticed** (they don't even know you're there). **Invisible** makes you undetected by sight no matter what you do. Stealth actions move you up and down this ladder.

## The Conditions

### Blinded
You can't see. All normal terrain is difficult terrain to you. You can't detect anything using vision. You automatically critically fail Perception checks that require you to be able to see, and if vision is your only precise sense, you take a –4 status penalty to Perception checks. You are immune to visual effects. Blinded overrides dazzled.

### Broken
Broken is a condition that affects objects, not creatures. A broken object can't be used for its normal function, and it doesn't grant bonuses. A broken weapon still counts as a weapon but takes a –2 item penalty to attack rolls made with it. Broken armor still grants its item bonus to AC, but it also imposes a status penalty to AC based on its category: –1 for light armor, –2 for medium armor, or –3 for heavy armor. Repairing an item (usually with a Crafting check) removes the broken condition.

### Clumsy
Your movements become clumsy and inexact. Clumsy always includes a value. You take a status penalty equal to the value to Dexterity-based checks and DCs, including AC, Reflex saves, ranged attack rolls, and Dexterity-based skill checks.

### Concealed
You are difficult for one or more creatures to see due to thick smoke, flickering shadows, or another form of obscurement. You can still be observed, but you're tougher to target: a creature that you're concealed from must succeed at a DC 5 flat check when targeting you with an attack, power, or other effect; on a failure, the effect doesn't affect you.

### Confused
You don't have your wits about you, and you attack wildly. You are off-guard, you don't treat anyone as your ally (though they might still treat you as theirs), and you can't Delay, Ready, or use reactions. You use all your actions to attack or harass whatever is near, using your simplest attacks and abilities; the GM determines your targets randomly, and if you have no other creature to attack, you lash out at objects or waste your turn raving. Each time you take damage from an attack or hostile ability, you can attempt a DC 11 flat check to recover your wits and end the condition.

### Controlled
Someone else is issuing your orders — through programming, domination of your mind, or a restraining bolt. The controller dictates how you act and can make you use any of your actions, including attacks, reactions, and even Speech, though it can generally direct only actions it knows you can take. The controlling effect states what happens if it forces you to act against your nature.

### Dazzled
Your eyes are overstimulated — glare, strobing light, or a flash grenade. If vision is your only precise sense, all creatures and objects are concealed from you.

### Deafened
You can't hear. You automatically critically fail Perception checks that require you to be able to hear, and you take a –2 status penalty to Perception checks for initiative and to checks that involve sound but also rely on other senses. If you perform an action with the Auditory trait, you must succeed at a DC 5 flat check or the action is lost. You are immune to auditory effects.

### Doomed
Your life is ebbing, drawn toward death. Doomed always includes a value. The dying value at which you die is reduced by your doomed value (so a doomed 1 character dies at dying 3, rather than dying 4). If your maximum dying value is ever reduced to 0, you instantly die. When you die, you're no longer doomed. Your doomed value decreases by 1 each time you get a full night's rest.

### Drained
Blood loss, life-draining powers, or organ trauma has sapped your vitality. Drained always includes a value. You take a status penalty equal to the value on Constitution-based checks, such as Fortitude saves. You also lose Hit Points equal to your level times the drained value, and your maximum Hit Points are reduced by the same amount (this Hit Point loss can't be healed while you remain drained, though the lost HP return as the condition fades). Your drained value decreases by 1 each time you get a full night's rest.

### Dying
You are bleeding out or otherwise at death's door. While dying, you are unconscious. Dying always includes a value, and if it ever reaches dying 4, you die. If you're dying, you must attempt a **recovery check** (a flat check with a DC of 10 + your current dying value) at the start of each of your turns to determine whether you get better or worse:

- **Critical Success** Your dying value is reduced by 2.
- **Success** Your dying value is reduced by 1.
- **Failure** Your dying value increases by 1.
- **Critical Failure** Your dying value increases by 2.

If you lose the dying condition by getting a success on a recovery check but are still at 0 Hit Points, you remain unconscious, and you can wake up as described in that condition. Anytime you lose the dying condition, you gain (or increase) the wounded condition by 1. If you take damage while dying, increase your dying value by 1 (or by 2 if the damage came from an enemy's critical hit or your own critical failure). When you gain the dying condition while you already have the wounded condition, increase your dying value by your wounded value.

### Encumbered
You are carrying more than you can manage. While encumbered, you're clumsy 1 and take a –10-foot penalty to all your Speeds. As with all penalties to your Speed, this can't reduce a Speed below 5 feet.

### Enfeebled
You're physically weakened. Enfeebled always includes a value. You take a status penalty equal to the value to Strength-based checks and DCs, including Strength-based melee attack rolls, Strength-based damage rolls, and Athletics checks.

### Fascinated
You are compelled to focus your attention on something. You take a –2 status penalty to Perception and skill checks, and you can't use actions with the Concentrate trait unless they (or their intended consequences) are related to the subject of your fascination, as determined by the GM. This condition ends if a creature takes hostile actions toward you or any of your allies.

### Fatigued
You're tired and can't summon much energy. You take a –1 status penalty to AC and saving throws. During exploration, you can't choose an exploration activity to perform while traveling. You recover from fatigue after a full night's rest.

### Fleeing
You're forced to run away. On your turns, you must spend each of your actions trying to escape the source of the condition as expediently as possible — usually by moving away or removing obstacles barring your escape. You can't Delay or Ready while fleeing. The effect that made you flee states how the condition ends.

### Frightened
You're gripped by fear and struggle to control your nerves. Frightened always includes a value. You take a status penalty equal to the value to all your checks and DCs. Unless specified otherwise, at the end of each of your turns, the value of your frightened condition decreases by 1.

### Glitching
Your systems are misfiring — feedback loops, scrambled sensors, corrupted routines. You take a –1 status penalty to your checks and DCs. Only creatures and objects with the Construct or Technological trait can be glitching. The effect that made you glitch states how long the condition lasts.

### Grabbed
You're held in place by another creature, giving you the off-guard and immobilized conditions. If you attempt an action with the Manipulate trait while grabbed, you must succeed at a DC 5 flat check or the action is lost. You usually end this condition with the Escape action.

### Hidden
While you're hidden from a creature, that creature knows the space you're in but can't tell precisely where you are. You typically become hidden by using Stealth to Hide. A creature you're hidden from is off-guard to you, and it must succeed at a DC 11 flat check when targeting you with an attack, power, or other effect or the attack or effect fails. A creature might be able to pinpoint you by Seeking.

### Immobilized
You're pinned, stuck, or held fast. You can't use any action with the Move trait. If you're immobilized by something holding you in place and an external force would move you out of your space, the force must succeed at a check against the DC of the effect holding you in place or the relevant defense of the creature holding you.

### Invisible
You can't be seen — a cloaking field, a bent-light trick of the Force. While invisible, you're undetected to everyone. Creatures can Seek to attempt to detect you; if a creature succeeds at its Perception check against your Stealth DC, you become hidden to that creature until you Sneak to become undetected again. If you become invisible while someone can already see you, you start out hidden to that observer (instead of undetected) until you successfully Sneak.

### Observed
You're in plain view — the default state for a creature in an open, well-lit space. Anything in plain sight is observed, and some abilities can be used only against an observed target. If a creature can't observe you (darkness, obstruction, its own blindness), you're at least concealed or hidden from it, per the detection ladder.

### Off-Guard
You're distracted, unprepared, or otherwise unable to focus on your defense. You take a –2 circumstance penalty to AC. Some effects give you the off-guard condition only to certain creatures or against certain attacks (a flanked creature is off-guard only to the flankers); others, such as prone or unconscious, apply it universally.

### Paralyzed
Your body is frozen in place. You have the off-guard condition and can't act except to take purely mental actions — thinking, recalling, and using abilities that require no motion or speech. Your senses still function, but only in the areas you can perceive without moving your body or head.

### Persistent Damage
You keep taking damage over time — burning plasma, arterial bleeding, corrosive spray. Persistent damage is written with its type, such as *2d4 persistent fire damage*. Instead of taking persistent damage immediately, you take it at the end of each of your turns as long as you have the condition, rolling any damage dice anew each time. After you take persistent damage, roll a DC 15 flat check to see if you recover: on a success, the condition ends. If you receive appropriate help — dousing flames, bandaging a wound, an ally's first aid — the DC is reduced to 10, or the help might end the condition automatically, as the GM determines. You can take multiple instances of persistent damage at once, but only one of each damage type; if you would gain a second instance of the same type, keep only the higher amount.

### Prone
You're lying on the ground. You are off-guard and take a –2 circumstance penalty to attack rolls. The only move actions you can use while prone are Crawl and Stand; Standing ends the prone condition. You can Take Cover while prone, hugging the ground to gain greater cover against ranged attacks (even if the terrain otherwise offers none).

### Quickened
You move with unnatural speed. You gain 1 additional action at the start of each of your turns. Many effects that make you quickened specify the types of action you can use the extra action for; if the effect doesn't say, you can use it for any single action. Because quickened has its effect when you regain actions, being quickened doesn't help you if you gain it partway through your turn.

### Restrained
You're tied up and can barely move, or a creature or effect has you completely pinned. You have the off-guard and immobilized conditions, and you can't use any actions with the Attack or Manipulate traits except to attempt to Escape or to break free of your bonds. Restrained overrides grabbed.

### Sickened
You feel ill. Sickened always includes a value. You take a status penalty equal to the value to all your checks and DCs, and you can't willingly ingest anything — including medicine and stims — while sickened. You can spend a single action retching in an attempt to recover, which lets you immediately attempt a Fortitude save against the DC of the effect that made you sickened; on a success, reduce your sickened value by 1 (by 2 on a critical success).

### Slowed
You have fewer actions than normal. Slowed always includes a value. When you regain your actions at the start of your turn, reduce the number of actions you regain by your slowed value. Because slowed has its effect at the start of your turn, becoming slowed partway through your turn doesn't cost you any of that turn's remaining actions.

### Stunned
You've become senseless — a blow to the head, a stun bolt, a psychic shock. You can't act while stunned, and you can't use reactions. Stunned usually includes a value: each time you regain actions at the start of your turn, reduce the number you regain by your stunned value, then reduce your stunned value by the number of actions you lost this way (so stunned 4 costs you a full three-action turn, then one more action the following turn). Stunned might instead state a duration, such as *stunned for 1 minute*; in that case you lose all your actions for the listed duration. Stunned overrides slowed: while you have both conditions, actions lost to stunned also count against slowed.

### Stupefied
Your thoughts are scattered and your judgment clouded. Stupefied always includes a value. You take a status penalty equal to the value to Intelligence-, Wisdom-, and Charisma-based checks and DCs, including Will saves, Perception checks, and skill checks using those attributes. Any time you attempt to use a Force power or activate a Tech Specialist Modification while stupefied, the effect is disrupted unless you succeed at a flat check with a DC of 5 + your stupefied value.

### Suppressed
You're keeping your head down under incoming fire. Suppressed always includes a value. You take a circumstance penalty to attack rolls equal to the condition value and a –10-foot status penalty to all your Speeds. If an effect makes you suppressed without listing a value, you're suppressed 1. The effect that suppressed you states how long the condition lasts. (Suppression is the signature verb of the Soldier class, whose features control where the condition applies and at what value; effects that ride along with suppression carry the **Suppressed** trait — see the Traits appendix.)

### Unconscious
You're sleeping, knocked out, or powered down. You can't act. You take a –4 status penalty to AC, Perception, and Reflex saves, and you have the blinded and off-guard conditions. When you gain this condition, you fall prone and drop items you're wielding or holding unless the effect states otherwise or the GM rules you're in a position not to.

**If you're unconscious because you're dying**, you can't wake up while you have 0 Hit Points. If you're restored to 1 Hit Point or more, you lose the dying and unconscious conditions and can act normally on your next turn.

**If you're unconscious at 0 Hit Points but not dying**, you return to 1 Hit Point and awaken after sufficient time passes, as determined by the GM. Taking damage while at 0 Hit Points makes you dying again.

**If you're unconscious with more than 0 Hit Points** (asleep, or knocked out by a nonlethal effect), you wake up when you take damage or receive healing, when loud noise erupts around you (though this might require a Perception check), or when someone spends an action to shake you awake. Simply resting ends this state when the GM determines you've slept enough.

### Undetected
A creature you're undetected by doesn't know what space you occupy. It can't target you, though it can try to guess your square and attack into it: it picks a square and attempts its attack, and the GM secretly rolls a DC 11 flat check — on a failure the attack misses outright, and on a success the attacker rolls against you normally, without knowing whether it chose the right square at all. The GM tracks your position in secret.

### Unnoticed
A creature that is entirely unaware of your presence finds you unnoticed. You are undetected by it, and it can't even attempt to guess where you are. Some abilities require you to be unnoticed by your target, not merely undetected.

### Wounded
You've been seriously injured and haven't fully stabilized. Wounded always includes a value. Any time you gain the dying condition while wounded, increase your dying value by your wounded value. Each time you lose the dying condition, your wounded value increases by 1. The wounded condition ends when someone successfully restores you with Treat Wounds (or Repair, for a droid), or when you're restored to full Hit Points and rest for 10 minutes.
